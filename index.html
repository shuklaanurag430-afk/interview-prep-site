Strategic Case Studies — With Sample Answers

Each case includes the problem statement, framework/approach, and a sample answer drawing on real Angel One experience.

Case 1: Acquisition costs are rising 30% YoY. Reduce blended CPA by 25%.

Scenario: You join as Head of Acquisition at Upstox. CPA has been climbing. CEO wants 25% reduction while maintaining volume.

Framework

1. DiagnoseSegment channels. Which is rising fastest? Fixed vs variable cost?
2. Funnel AnalysisImpressions → clicks → signups → KYC → funded → traded. Where is leakage?
3. Identify LeversOrganic (brand, referrals, SEO) · Paid retargeting · Payout restructuring · Product-led growth · Inactive base reactivation
4. PrioritizeWhich 2-3 levers have highest impact with lowest implementation cost?
Sample Answer:

I would start by segmenting acquisition costs by channel to identify the biggest offenders. At Angel One, I found that volume DRAs on fixed payouts (₹250/kyc) were our highest cost with near-zero return — 90% of FYR came from 10% of KYCs. We restructured to a self-selection quality-tier model.

For Upstox, the approach would be similar:
1. Disaggregate: Split CPA by channel — paid search, social, organic, referrals, affiliate. Find which channel has the worst cost-to-revenue ratio.
2. Reallocate budget: Shift spend toward channels with better unit economics. Kill or cap underperforming channels.
3. Product-led growth: Build referral loops into the product. Drop a "Refer a friend — earn ₹200" nudge right after a user completes their first trade.
4. Retarget: Users who signed up but never funded are a free acquisition channel. Re-engage via email/SMS with a small incentive.
5. Re-activate: Inactive clients are 3-5x cheaper to re-activate than acquire new ones. Run targeted campaigns.

At Angel One we reduced CPA from ₹423 to ₹202 — a 52% reduction — primarily by restructuring offers based on DRA performance tier, not across-the-board cuts.
Interview Tip: Lead with data — mention the 90/10 rule (90% of revenue from 10% of sources). Use the actual ₹423→₹202 trajectory as evidence.

Case 2: Design an acquisition channel from scratch — partner/referral program at Zerodha

Scenario: Zerodha wants to launch a partner/referral channel for the first time. Target: 50K new clients/quarter. How do you build it?

Framework

1. Channel StructureDirect referral? Tiered partner system? Agency model?
2. Payout DesignFixed per client? Brokerage sharing? Hybrid? SEBI compliance.
3. Quality GuardrailsFake activation prevention, minimum KYC quality, verification
4. Tech StackTracking infra, dashboard, automated payouts, real-time reporting
5. Scale PathGrow from 0 → 50 → 500 → 5000 partners with systems intact
6. Unit EconomicsTarget CPA, COA, activation rate. What is viable for Zerodha?
Sample Answer:

Zerodha has never had a referral channel, which is both a risk and an opportunity. Here is how I would approach it:

Phase 1 — Design (Month 1-2):
• Structure: Two-tier model — direct referral (existing clients refer friends) + partner tier (influencers/content creators with 10K+ followers). Direct referral gets a one-time ₹200 credit. Partners get ₹200 fixed + 10% of referred client first 3 months brokerage.
• Compliance: No revenue sharing promises. Fixed payout structure only — SEBI-compliant since Aug 2024 circular.
• Quality: Minimum hold period (client must trade ₹1,000+ to count). Verification via social media for partners.

Phase 2 — Pilot (Month 3):
• Seed with top 500 Kite users (highest engagement, lower churn)
• Track: referral conversion rate, CPA, activation rate, 30-day retention
• Target metrics: 15%+ referral conversion, CPA ≤ ₹150, activation ≥ 60%

Phase 3 — Scale (Month 4-6):
• Open to all users with in-app referral flows
• Launch partner tier — onboard 50-100 influencers in first quarter
• Target: 12,500 clients/month from referrals = 50K/quarter

The key lesson from Angel One: payout structure drives behavior. If you pay flat per KYC, partners optimize for volume, not quality. Structure payouts to reward quality — client activation, first trade, continued trading. Zerodha's brand trust would give them a significant advantage — partners would accept lower upfront payouts for the Zerodha brand association.

Case 3: Activation rate is 2%. How do you get it to 15%?

Scenario: Motilal Oswal launched a new discount brokerage product. Users sign up but don't place their first trade. Activation rate is 2%. What's the strategy?

Framework

1. Define ActivationWhat is the 'Aha!' moment? First trade? First deposit? First ₹10K portfolio?
2. Diagnose Drop-offFunnel: Registration → KYC → Funding → First Trade. Where do most users drop?
3. Identify LeversProduct onboarding, incentives, education, RM follow-up, platform UX
4. Cohort AnalysisWeek 1 vs Week 4 activation. Which channels have highest activation?
Sample Answer:

At Angel One, I faced this exact problem — DRA activation was under 1% in Sep 25. We increased it to ~7% by Dec 25. The playbook:

Step 1: Diagnose the bottleneck
We traced new DRA behavior and found they weren't understanding the payout model — they saw '35% brokerage sharing' but couldn't visualize their earnings. The problem wasn't willingness, it was comprehension.

Step 2: Targeted intervention — activation webinars
We launched live educational webinars that simulated earnings — "If you bring 10 clients with ₹200 average FYR, you earn ₹700 in fixed payout + ₹700 in brokerage share = ₹1,400 total." Made the math tangible.

Step 3: Simplified first action
Instead of asking new DRAs to "go get clients," we asked them to "share your referral link with 3 friends today." Much lower barrier. Once they saw the first KYC come through, they were hooked.

Step 4: RM follow-up protocol
New DRAs got a WhatsApp message on Day 1, a call on Day 3, and payout reminder on Day 7. Structured cadence, not random outreach.

Step 5: First-trade bonus
"Bring 3 clients who each place one trade — earn ₹500 bonus." Incentivized both acquisition and activation.

For Motilal Oswal's retail product, I would apply similar thinking but adapted for end-users: virtual portfolio mode to practice first, a guided first trade experience (buy ₹500 of NIFTYBEES), and a first-trade reward (₹100 free brokerage credit upon completion).
Sample answer draws directly from real work — activation from less than 1% to ~7% at Angel One. Concrete numbers make the answer credible.

Case 4: Groww launched MTF. Zerodha's response?

Scenario: You're at Zerodha. Groww's MTF product reached ₹1,036 Cr book. Zerodha already has MTF. How do you defend/win?

Sample Answer:

Let me contextualize the MTF market first: Industry MTF book is ₹84,659 Cr (Jun 25), growing 25% YoY. Groww has 1.22% market share — they're small but growing fast. Dhan has the lowest rate at 12.49%.

Zerodha's advantages: Kite is the #1 trading platform. Our user base is more trading-oriented than Groww's (Groww started as MF platform, their users lean investing). MTF is more relevant for traders than investors.

The strategy:
1. Price optimization: Match Dhan's 12.49% for high-volume traders. Keep standard 14.6% for retail. Don't need to blanket-cut — use smart segmentation.
2. Feature differentiation: More stock eligibility (Groww lists ~200, Zerodha can do 500+). Better MTF analytics in Kite — show users exactly how much they'd earn vs cost.
3. Bundle: Zerodha MTF + Kite API = powerful for algo traders. Build a "leverage optimizer" tool that suggests optimal MTF usage based on trading patterns.
4. Education moat: Varsity already has MTF explainers. Create Varsity module: "When to use MTF vs when to avoid it." Builds trust and positions Zerodha as the responsible broker.

Long-term: MTF is a commoditizing product. Price will converge. The moat is the ecosystem — Kite + Varsity + API + community. Don't win on MTF price, win on the platform.

Case 5: SEBI tightened F&O. Volumes down 35%. Growth plan?

Scenario: Industry F&O notional ADTO contracted 38% (Jun 24-Jun 25). Your firm is F&O-heavy. How do you pivot growth strategy?

Sample Answer:

This is real — the SEBI tightening has already hit. Individual investor F&O participation is down 36% in the same period. Here's the playbook:

1. Revenue rebalancing (short-term): Push delivery volumes. Launch MTF bonus campaigns. Increase mutual fund distribution revenue. Float income becomes more important.
2. Product expansion (medium-term): Wealth management, PMS-lite, international investing, bond market access. Broaden the revenue base beyond F&O.
3. Client conversion (ongoing): Convert F&O traders to longer-term delivery investors. Run campaigns: "From speculative to systematic — build your portfolio."
4. Cost structure (immediate): F&O revenue was subsidizing low-margin acquisition. Without F&O volumes, CPA targets need to be more aggressive. Revisit payout structures.
5. Acquisition focus shift: Acquire investors, not traders. Long-term investors have lower churn and build AUM over time. Adjust marketing messaging from "lowest brokerage" to "build wealth."

The industry is moving from a volume game (F&O turnover) to a value game (AUM, client lifetime value). Brokers who adapt faster will win the next phase.

Product & Feature Thinking — With Sample Answers

Product design and feature strategy questions focused on acquisition, activation, and retention.

Q1: Design a referral program for Upstox — under ₹30 CPA, SEBI-compliant, viral loop

Sample Answer:

Let me design this in tiers:

Tier 1 — User Referral (Viral):
• Existing user refers a friend → both get ₹200 free brokerage credit (vested over 3 months of active trading)
• CPA: ₹400/2 = ₹200 per referred client (but only if both activate) — effectively ₹50 if 75% drop off
• SEBI compliance: Fixed reward, not revenue sharing. No promises about future earnings.
• Viral loop: "Share and earn" button after every successful trade

Tier 2 — Influencer Program:
• Finance content creators with 5K+ followers get ₹200 per KYC + 5% of referred brokerage for 6 months
• CPA: ₹200 + variable brokerage share (capped at ₹50/client)
• Compliance: Fixed payout is disclosed. Variable share is documented as performance bonus.
• Onboarding: Automated link generation, real-time dashboard

Why this works for Upstox:
• Upstox has brand recognition (Ratan Tata backing) — influencers want association
• ₹200 fixed + brokerage share beats most competitors on total earning potential
• Viral loop through trading moments creates organic growth

Target economics:
• Blended CPA: ₹150-200
• Activation rate: 60%+
• Break-even on client: Month 3-4 (vs industry avg 6-8 months)

Q2: Improve Zerodha's client onboarding funnel — currently drops 80%+ of users

Sample Answer:

Kite is best-in-class for existing traders. The problem is getting new users past the KYC-funding-first trade barrier. Here's the fix:

1. UPI auto-funding integration: Right after KYC approval, show a "Add funds" screen with UPI auto-pay. Don't make users switch apps. Pre-fill with ₹500 — one tap funding.
2. Guided first trade: A "First Trade" wizard that walks through buying 1 unit of NIFTYBEES (₹200-250). Real trade, real money, zero risk. Once they've done it once, they're 3x more likely to trade again.
3. "Try before you buy" mode: Virtual ₹1L portfolio for 7 days. Users can practice trading without risk. After 7 days, prompt: "Ready to go live? Your ₹500 bonus is waiting."
4. In-app video KYC: Don't redirect to external site. Complete KYC start-to-finish in Kite app.
5. Referral frictional drop: If the user came via referral, pre-fill their first ₹500 deposit with the referrer's bonus — makes the connection tangible.

Expected impact: 80% → 50% drop (still high, but 3x more activated users without incremental acquisition spend, just product improvement).

Q3: Design a feature to reduce inactive users — 60% inactive within 6 months

Sample Answer:

60% inactive by 6 months means the product isn't integrating into users' financial lives. The fix isn't a single feature — it's a re-engagement system:

Core Feature: "My Portfolio Health" Dashboard
• Shows: missed gains (if you'd invested ₹10K in Reliance 6 months ago...), dividend alerts, portfolio diversification score
• If portfolio is below ₹1K: "Add ₹5,000 and you could earn ~₹200/month in dividends" — aspiration, not obligation

Trigger-Based Re-engagement (automated):
• Day 30 inactivity: Email — "Stock you watched (RELIANCE) is up 12%"
• Day 60: SMS — "Your portfolio returned -2% this month → here's a suggested rebalance"
• Day 90: WhatsApp — "₹500 free brokerage credit waiting. Unlock by placing one trade."

Dream Goal Tracker:
• User sets a goal: "I want ₹5L for my child's education in 5 years"
• Kite shows: "You're ₹8,000 away from this month's target. Invest ₹2,000/month to stay on track."
• Progress bar creates stickiness — humans hate losing progress

News-Based Nudge:
• "Reliance just announced a buyback. You can participate..." → creates urgency and action
• Personalized: The system learns what stocks the user watches and creates relevant alerts

Expected impact: 60% → 40% inactive (33% reduction in churn). Revenue impact: Significant from retained inactive users who start trading again.

Behavioral & Experience Questions — With Sample Answers

STAR-format answers mapped to actual accomplishments. Practice telling these stories naturally.

Q1: Tell me about a time you made a data-driven decision that was unpopular

Situation: Angel One's DRA channel had 70,000+ registered DRAs, but deep analysis showed 90% of FYR came from just 10% of DRAs. The remaining 90% were cost centers — earning fixed payouts with near-zero client trading activity.

Task: I needed to clean up the channel to improve unit economics. But terminating DRAs meant losing potential future volume.

Action: I ran a full-segment analysis (lifetime tier + current state + trend). Then systematically terminated inactive/low-quality DRAs, reducing the base from 70,000 to 2,400. I restructured payouts — quality-tier options instead of flat-rate for everyone. For volume DRAs earning ₹250/kyc with zero FYR, I moved them to a self-selection model: ₹100 + 45% brokerage (quality-aligned) or ₹250 + 35% (status quo).

Result: Channel quality transformed. CPA dropped 52% (₹423 → ₹202). Activation improved from less than 1% to ~7%. The 2,400 remaining DRAs produced better quality clients than the 70,000 did before.

Q2: Tell me about managing conflicting stakeholders (FDS vs Business)

Situation: FDS (Fraud Detection & Surveillance) would send show-cause emails to DRAs for compliance violations. DRAs would respond/remediate, but FDS would terminate them anyway. The business team lost valuable contributors. CXO had to personally intervene for every save.

Task: Create a process that satisfies both FDS (compliance safety) and business (revenue retention).

Action: Mapped 7 specific process gaps. Designed a joint review workflow: FDS evaluates compliance → business evaluates revenue impact → joint decision. Added case closure confirmation for DRAs, offense counter reset after remediation, and clear escalation criteria (only top-tier DRAs go to CXO). Standardized email templates with specific violations so DRAs knew exactly what to fix.

Result: Process adopted. FDS no longer overrides remediated cases. Business has a formal seat at the table. CXO involvement reduced by ~70%.

Q3: How did you achieve a stretch target with limited resources?

Situation: The RM team was reduced from 7 to 3 people (budget cuts), while the DRA channel needed more attention — onboarding, top DRA engagement, dormant reactivation.

Task: Achieve better channel outcomes with less than half the RM headcount.

Action: Built structured systems to multiply RM productivity: • DRA Pulse Programme — Weekly strategic syncs with top performers only. Created direct feedback loop. • Activation webinars — Instead of 1:1 calls for every new DRA, ran group webinars. Core content: how brokerage sharing works, earnings simulation. Scaled 1 RM → 50 DRAs per session. • Re-engagement campaigns — Automated dormant DRA outreach (WhatsApp at Day 15, call at Day 30) so RMs only engaged warm leads. • Categorized DRA list — Top 53 → monthly; 300+ offer DRAs → bi-weekly; new DRAs → weekly. Clear cadence for each tier.

Result: Activation increased from less than 1% to ~7%. Top DRAs engaged more consistently. 3 RMs achieved what 7 couldn't — because the process was systematized.

Q4: Tell me about a time you found a problem nobody was looking at

Situation: While analyzing DRA operations, I discovered that DRAs had direct access to client PII — PAN numbers and mobile numbers. This was a massive compliance and reputational risk. No one had flagged it.

Task: Eliminate client data exposure immediately.

Action: Worked with engineering to revoke DRA system access to PAN and mobile fields. Implemented 100% mask — DRAs could still see KYC counts and client codes but not personal information. Audited all existing systems to ensure no backdoor access remained.

Result: Achieved 100% data privacy compliance. Risk of unauthorized DRA-to-client contact eliminated. This was later used as a case study for internal data security practices.

Q5: Tell me about a strategic decision that failed and what you learned

Situation: Volume DRAs (100+ KYCs/month) were on ₹250 fixed per KYC + 35% brokerage sharing. They were our highest-volume producers. But their clients had near-zero FYR — they were bringing dead accounts via affiliate links on WhatsApp/Telegram.

Task: Convert volume to quality without losing them.

What I tried (that partially failed): I restructured their payouts — reduced fixed to ₹200 with quality bonus for client activation. The volume DRAs stopped producing. Volume dropped 40% in one month.

What I learned: Volume DRAs had a business model — post affiliate links → earn per KYC. They weren't interested in quality because their audience was volume-based. Cutting their payout didn't change behavior, it killed the channel.

Pivot: Switched to self-selection — let DRAs choose their own payout structure. Offered ₹100 + 45% (quality path) or ₹250 + 35% (volume path). Those who chose quality started delivering better clients. Those who chose volume continued but at least now the cost was justified. The lesson: don't force change, let partners opt-in to better structures.

Q6: How do you think about competitive positioning?

Situation: Angel One's DRA payout (₹100-400 + 35%) was significantly lower than competitors (Upstox ₹600+40%, Dhan 65%, Samco ₹700+90%). DRAs were leaving for better offers.

Task: Position Angel One to win on non-price factors.

Approach: 1. Brand leverage: Angel One is a 29-year-old listed brand. Trust matters for DRAs who refer their personal network. 2. Brokerage sharing education: Most DRAs only look at fixed payout. We showed them: 35% of ₹5,000 average FYR = ₹1,750 per client. Over 10 clients = ₹17,500/month. Made the variable component tangible. 3. Platform differentiation: Angel One's NXT dashboard gives DRAs real-time payout tracking. Competitors often delay payout visibility. 4. Targeted offers: Instead of blanket increases, gave higher offers only to proven performance tiers. 23 DRAs on 300+ offer, top performers on ₹400 + 45%.

Result: Retained top performers despite lower headline payouts. Discovery: most DRAs optimize for total income, not per-unit payout. If their clients earn more at Angel One (better brokerage), their share is higher. Sold the value of the ecosystem, not the upfront payment.

Q7: What's your approach to building something from zero?

Situation: Angel One's DRA channel relied entirely on organic listing. No agency model, no performance marketing, no self-serve registration. I needed to create new acquisition engines.

Approach: 1. Agency Model: Partnered with 5 specialized agencies that already had DRA networks. Gave them batch onboarding, centralized tracking, and bulk payout mechanisms. These agencies brought pre-qualified DRAs — higher activation from day one. 2. Performance Marketing (UAC + Meta): Launched Google UAC campaigns targeting "become a DRA" and "referral partner" keywords. Meta campaigns targeted finance-content followers. Key learning: these channels work for top-of-funnel awareness but CPA is higher than organic. 3. Website Self-Registration: New "Become a DRA" page with pre-qualification form — social media handles, expected volume, content style. Auto-route high-quality leads to RM for fast onboarding.

Result: 3 new channels live within 3 months. Agency model scaling fastest. Self-serve registration capturing low-volume but high-intent partners. Performance marketing still optimizing for CPA.

Market & Industry Q&A

Quick-fire questions testing your understanding of the Indian broking landscape.

What is the biggest threat to discount brokers today?

F&O regulatory tightening (38% volume contraction). Discount brokers earn disproportionately from F&O volumes. SEBI's measures to reduce retail participation in derivatives directly impact revenue models. The pivot to AUM-based revenue (delivery, MF, wealth) will determine who survives the next 12-18 months.

How does Groww's IPO change the competitive landscape?

Groww now has ~₹3,500 Cr from the IPO to invest in brand building, MTF, NBFC, and tech. Combined with 26% market share and 83% organic acquisition, they can afford to not compete on price. Their DRHP revealed 77.7% 3-year retention — they're sticky. Competition just got a well-funded, already-dominant player.

Why is Dhan growing faster than Zerodha despite being 10 years younger?

Dhan entered with a clean slate — no legacy systems, aggressive pricing (12.49% MTF, ₹0 delivery, ₹0 lifetime AMC), modern tech (free TradingView, Fuzz AI, copy trading). Zerodha is managing a declining base because they haven't innovated on acquisition. Dhan is eating Zerodha's lunch on pricing and tech maturity.

Will a partner/referral channel work for Zerodha?

Yes, if positioned correctly. Zerodha's brand trust means partners would accept lower payouts for the association. The challenge: Zerodha's culture has always been product-led, not sales-led. Building a partner channel would require organizational change. But if they do it right, they could capture the mid-tier influencer market that Angel One currently owns.

Which product feature would have the biggest impact on reducing churn?

A personalized portfolio tracker with goal-based investing. The #1 reason users churn is they don't see their money growing and lose interest. If you show them a goal (retirement, education, vacation) with a progress bar and automated SIP suggestions, they stay invested. Robinhood proved this — users with goals have 3x higher retention.

What would you do differently if you were leading growth at Upstox today?

Upstox has brand recognition (Ratan Tata) but hasn't converted it to acquisition leadership. I'd launch a referral program (₹200/referred + brokerage share), reactivate their dormant user base (estimated 2-3M inactive accounts), and double down on MTF with competitive pricing. Their current MTF rate of ~18.25% is the highest in the market — bringing it to 14% would unlock significant volume.
